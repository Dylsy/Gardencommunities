1. Strategic site selection and initial visioning

The first step in your masterplanning process is to do a thorough strategic site selection process.

This will involve identifying potential growth sites. Also you;ll be appraising the spatial constraints and opportunities of each site against the overall spatial strategy and sustainability objectives for the wider plan making area.

While this stage focusses more on vision, masterplan optioneering and capacity studies can be undertaken to inform the concept plan.

Identifying a location / assessing the right approach

  • location for a garden community can be identified either through a broad location or strategic site allocation in the emerging Local Plan
  • before a broad location or strategic site is selected for allocation as a garden community in the local plan, a full assessment of alternative sites should be undertaken in accordance with the Environmental Assessment of Plans and Programmes 2004.
  • Your sustainability apÂ­praisal of the plan should include the sustainability assessment of reasonable alternative sites. This work should be fed into the options consultation <Site assessment link>
  • the reasoning for why alternative locations/ sites were discounted and the preferred site chosen needs to be clearly set out.

2. Concept masterplan

The concept masterplan sets out the key structuring principles of a masterplan for a garden community

  • it reflects the overall spatial vision, development objectives, spatial parameters, design and delivery principles.
  • the concept masterplan document should explain the sitesâ€™ constraints, opportunities and development capacity. It sets out high-level spatial parameters for the location, scale and type of land uses, green infrastructure, movement and strategic urban design principles (gateways, community hubs, important frontages, and sensitive areas) which form a framework for development. Your masterplan will clarify the critical infrastructure requirements, strategic mitigaÂ­tion measures and includes a viability assessment and a delivery strategy for the site.

Establishing the policy basis for a garden community

  • collate an evidence base for the site to support site-specific policies in the development plan, either as a broad location or strategic site allocation.
  • test and evaluate alternative options as part of the design process
  • demonstrate the site is viable and deliverable and there are nothing that could prevent development coming forward in the planned timescale. If a site is to be brought forward early in the plan period then a greater level of detail (and certainty about how it is to be delivered) is likely to be required.

Level of detail needed

  • provide clarity on spatial parameters (or frameworks) for the following elements of development:
    • movement and access
    • built up areas
    • nodes and landmarks
    • green infrastructure
    • key contextual interface
    • high-level viability

This can be an effective approach (particularly where there are multiple land interests) to support the integration of development proposals and co-ordination, and funding and delivery of social and physical infrastructure, thus speeding up implementation.

Landowners/developers can work collaboratively to prepare a masterplan framework in advance of outline planning applications to help speed up delivery and support comprehensive development.

This would provide further detail on the anticipated layout and development form by identifying a development block structure, a green infrastructure network and street hierarchy and urban design principles.

The masterplan framework would provide clarity on the phasing, funding and delivery of social and physical infrastructure requirements to support the development. Where there are multiple landowners this would include setting out proportionate contributions to the site wide infrastructure requirements.

Further site specific masterplanning guidance for a garden community prepared and adopted either as a supplementary planning document (SPD) or a Development Plan Document (DPD).

  • DPDs typically follow a broad location allocation in the Local Plan. SPDs would more typically follow a strategic site allocation.
  • The Council would lead on the consultation of this document. Due to its site-focused nature it offers opportunities to engage the community and stakeholders in the development proposals to inform subsequent planning applications.

Level of detail

A framework masterplan covers the following areas:

  • movement and access
  • built up areas
  • nodes and landmarks
  • green infrastructure
  • key contextual interfaces
  • land uses and capacities
  • phasing and viability
  • environmental assets
  • implementation framework

** Outline planning application/parameters and principles document**

A more detailed (potentially focussing on a particular phase) masterplan can be produced to further resolve design issues at the block, street and public realm scale and agree a basis for outline or full applications.

A detailed masterplan covers the following additional areas:

  • details of character area(s)

  • detailed design codes
  • delivery approach

4. Detailed masterplan

An outline application needs to provide parameter plans and typically be supported by written documents including:

  • design and access statement

  • an engagement, social development and stewardship strategy (including governance arrangements)

  • an economic development strategy

  • an energy strategy

  • a green infrastructure strategy that includes play and recreation

  • a transport strategy

  • a cultural strategy

  • The fundamental masterplan structuring elements (land use, green infrastructure, movement and urban design principles) can be graphically annotated by plans and diagrams and fixed by the outline permission to secure design quality in more detailed masterplans/planning applications. <Add pictures?>

  • Such parameters would also be supported by clear design principles to provide design guidance for further masterplanning, this can refer to illustrative material in the design and access statement. It can effectively be conditioned to the outline permission as a clear reference document for more detailed site planning.

After the grant of outline planning permission, work is then undertaken to prepare more detailed designs for development, prior to submission of reserved matters applications.

This stage is an extension of the detailed masterplanning stage. It allows more detailed work to be done before to submission of reserved matters.

It can be the stage when housebuilders/ developers become more involved in the design process and aspects of the proposed development form.

  • this should be a collaborative process, working with all stakeholders to further develop designs, within the bounds of the planning permission.
  • Masterplanning conditions attached to the outline planning permission should set out a clear and effective masterplanning approval process. This can require area-based/phased masterplans, design codes, design briefs or further design detail of strategic spatial components (like main street, strategic park, primary school, neighbourhood centre) to be approved in advance of reserved matters.
  • often strategic and site-wide unifying elements of a masterplan span different ownÂ­erships. Approving a clear design strategy for these development components will avoid the defaulting to a â€˜standardsâ€™ driven approach and support integration and distinctiveness.
  • reserved Matters submissions will need to be in accordance with any area-based/phased masterplans, design codes, design briefs or further design detail of strategic spatial components.
  • applications will include detailed proposals for the development parcels. Councils will expect all reserved matters applications to demonstrate compliance with the various layers of master planning work described above. Issues relating to management and maintenance are key here too.

How to develop and test your masterplan

What to consider when developing and testing a masterplan for a garden community.

  • masterplan must be consistent with the vision, principles and objectives established for the garden community. Are there gaps where interpretation of a principle or objective is not clear? Are there areas that require further refinement?

  • engagement with local people and stakeholders to feed into the evolution of the masterplan. Has this included diverse and traditionally hard to reach groups that form part of the local community? How have views been reflected?

  • Describe the new garden community distinctive qualities. Are the components that will make it distinctive clearly set out?

  • mix of uses must be supported by robust evidence base relating to needs and requirements. How does the arrangement of land uses proposed reinforce the creation of lively mixed-use neighbourhoods and easy sustainable travel between uses?

  • provision for local employment opportunities needs to be been included. How does this take account of likely changes to future working patterns? How will sustainable travel between homes and jobs be encouraged?

  • Understand the target modal shift for the garden community. What measures have been integrated into the masterplan to help achieve that target? What measures are proposed to connect the new community to its hinterland and local networks?

  • overarching landscape framework needs to respond to the landscape character assessments and provide a legible, connected and cohesive context for and in your garden community

  • masterplan must address the overall place-making structure to ensure it has a heart and connected** neighbourhoods.** How is the green infrastructure and built environment integrated to create a beautiful environment?

  • masterplan should set out clearly what the key fixes are. This includes â€“ fixed and variable elements.

  • Sustainability objectives which are integrated into the masterplan to create a resilient place. This can include landscape and the provision of a comprehensive green and blue infrastructure network, habitat creation, net biodiversity gain, and innovative approaches to energy, waste and water supply and usage

  • your masterplan must identify the infrastructure required to support your garden community. It must provide detail on its location, design, phasing (trigger points) and delivery mechanism. And infrastructure delivery plan needs to be in place.

  • address the phasing and implementation of development of the garden community. Think about equalisation issues in the allocation of land uses and phasing of infrastructure. Is there a coherent framework in place and is there support for this from the landowners/developers?
